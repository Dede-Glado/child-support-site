How child support is actually calculated
Every U.S. state uses one of three calculation models. Knowing which one applies to you helps you understand the outcome and challenge it if needed.
Used in 40+ states
Income Shares Model
FL, TX, NY, IL, GA, VA, AZ, CO, NC and most others
Combines both parents' incomes to determine a total child support obligation — as if the family were still together. Each parent then pays a proportional share based on their income percentage. The non-custodial parent typically pays their share directly to the custodial parent.
~6 states
Percentage of Income Model
Alaska, Mississippi, North Dakota, Texas (some courts), Wisconsin
Support is calculated as a fixed percentage of only the paying parent's income. Your income is not factored in. The percentage increases with the number of children — for example, 17% for 1 child, 25% for 2 children in Texas.
3 states
Melson Formula
Delaware, Hawaii, Montana
A more complex version that first ensures each parent's basic needs are met before calculating support. Then it applies a percentage of remaining income to the child's needs, with a "standard of living" adjustment that benefits the child as income increases.
